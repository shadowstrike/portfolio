Overview

JIRA Software teams were managing external requests (feature requests, bugs, customer inquiries) through fragmented channels—Slack messages, emails, verbal conversations, and multiple 3rd-party tools like ServiceDesk, Zoho, and Freshdesk. This created visibility gaps, context loss, and disconnected workflows between product managers and developers.

I designed a native "Requests" feature within JSW that eliminated the need for external request management tools, enabling seamless request intake directly from customers while providing automatic progress visibility through automated emails and a dedicated request portal.

Context & Challenge

The Problem Space

For JIRA Software (JSW) customers, requests originated from two sources:

  • Internal: Generated by Product Owners for feature development, platform enhancements, or UI improvements
  • External: From other teams or customers in the form of feature requests or bugs

Critical Pain Points

  1. Fragmented intake channels: Requests came through Slack, Email, or verbal conversations—creating tracking chaos
  2. Tool accessibility gaps: Product managers used 3rd-party apps (ServiceDesk, Zoho, Freshdesk) that developers couldn't access
  3. Context loss: Requests relayed by PMs often lacked complete information from the original requester
  4. Zero visibility: Requesters had no way to track progress without manually asking
  5. Incomplete information: Development teams struggled to understand the full problem due to missing context

Key Insights from Customer Interviews

  • Limited information visibility: Users couldn't see who requested what, when—creating accountability gaps
  • Request management using archival processes: Teams relied on outdated methods rather than modern workflow tools
  • Insufficient context for prioritization: Lack of request details made sprint planning difficult
  • Feedback loops broken: No mechanism to inform requesters of status changes or completion
  • Lack of Request transparency: Requesters felt disconnected from the development process
  • Complete disconnection: Customers couldn't track requests; no visibility into what happens after submission

Solution Design

Target Cohort

Our primary users were Supervisors—those in manager or lead positions with a supervisor mindset. This included:

  • Engineering managers
  • Team leads
  • Project managers
  • Product managers

These users were responsible for planning team work, reviewing team performance, and reporting to stakeholders.

Supervisor persona profile and key characteristics

Design Hypotheses

Design hypotheses and goals for the request management feature

Goals for Supervisors

  • Enabling teams to submit requests: Provide a friction-free submission process that respects the team's choice of tools and increases visibility
  • Making it easier to capture unforeseen work: Allow teams to log ad-hoc work that appears mid-sprint without disrupting current workflows
  • Supervisors value and want to understand requests properly: Surface request context to help supervisors make informed prioritization decisions
  • Product Manager's care depends on it focusing on the highest value requests: Provide filtering and prioritization tools
  • Managers could track need visibility into how work is going for the team they are tracking: Real-time dashboards and status updates
  • We are not supporting other team for request types to enable use case outside of work (i.e ITSD): Focus specifically on engineering request workflows

Impact & Results

Feature Utilization Increase

Achieved 30% increase in feature utilization with 80% of the sample size (100 customers) actively engaging in the first 2 weeks post-launch, demonstrating immediate product-market fit.

User Retention

Secured 25% retention rate of users who adopted the feature during the test phase. Established target to increase retention to 65% within the next three months through iterative improvements.

Customer Satisfaction

Earned CSAT score of 3 out of 5 based on post-implementation surveys. Developed action plan to achieve and maintain CSAT score of 4+ through targeted UX enhancements and user feedback integration.

Action Plans for Improvement

1. Enhance Feature Discoverability

  • Conduct targeted user outreach to understand factors driving engagement
  • Implement user-friendly tooltips or tutorials to enhance feature discoverability
  • Collaborate with UX/UI team to refine the feature based on user feedback

2. Improve Retention Rates

  • Analyze user behavior through analytics tools to identify drop-off points
  • Implement targeted communication campaigns to re-engage users who show declining activity

3. Boost Customer Satisfaction

  • Analyze customer support tickets and address reported issues promptly
  • Implement improvements based on customer feedback, focusing on pain points
  • Communicate updates and enhancements to users through release notes and newsletters

Key Learnings

Design for Continuous Iteration

The design process didn't end at launch. The action plans were designed to address potential challenges, enhance discoverability, and promptly respond to user feedback, ensuring that the feature's positive momentum is sustained over time.

Metrics Drive Improvement

Regular engagement monitoring, user feedback analysis, and iterative improvements were essential in maintaining and enhancing the success of the design feature. Key results aligned with metrics that directly impact the user experience, including feature utilization, NPS, retention rates, and CSAT scores.

Build on Existing Patterns

By leveraging familiar JSM service desk patterns within JSW, we reduced the learning curve for users already familiar with Atlassian products, accelerating adoption.
